Grammar: modal verbs

What are modal verbs? How are they different from 'normal' verbs?

Modal verbs are used to show modality -  likelihood, ability, permission, request, capacity, suggestions, order, obligation, or advice.

Modal verbs in English are the following;

can - could
may - might
will - would
shall - should
must

There are also semi-modals that are like normal verbs in some ways, but like modal verbs in other ways.

dare
used to
ought to
need 

Modal verbs are different from other verbs because:
  • They don't use the '-s' ending for the third person singular form in the present simple (the present simple is the same for all subjects). 
  • They don't use helping verbs to make negatives or questions (we only need 'not' to make a negative). 
  • They make questions by changing the order of the words ('She can swim' -> 'Can she swim?').
  • They are followed directly by the bare infintive of another verb (without 'to').
  • They don't have continuous forms. 
  • Many of them do not have the past or future forms.

We will now take a look at the usage of modals in more detail.

As mentioned, modal verbs are used to express ability, permission, request, likelihood, capacity, suggestions, order, obligation, or advice.

Ability
CAN, COULD
Semi-modal: be able to

I can run very fast.
I could run faster if I practiced.
When I was young I could not run so fast.
I will be able to help you after I finish.

You can read more about modals of ability HERE and then do some practice exercises HERE.

Permission and request
MAY, CAN, COULD
Semi-modals: be allowed to

May I open the window?
Can I go now?
Could you help me?
You are not allowed to go there!

You can read more about modals of permission HERE and do some practice exercises HERE.

Obligation and necessity
MUST, SHOULD
Semi-modals: ought to, have to, need to

You must clean up the mess you made. = You have to clean up.
You must not make a mess. = You are not allowed to make a mess.
You don't have to clean it up. = There is no obligation to clean it up.
You need to clean it up. = It is necessary that you do it.
You need not clean it up. = It is not necessary for you to do it.

You can read more about modals of permission, prohibition and obligation and do some practice exercises HERE.

Advice
SHOULD
Semi-modals: ought to, had better

You should see a doctor. 
You ought to see a doctor.
You had better see a doctor.

You can read more about modals of advice and do some practice exercises HERE.

Likelihood
WILL, MAY, MIGHT, CAN, COULD, MUST

It will rain today. = It's a fact.
It may/can start raining soon. = It is possible.
It might/could rain today. = It is somewhat likely. 
It might not rain this week. = It is possible it will not rain.
It cannot be cold outside. = It is unlikely it is cold.
You must be cold. = I am sure you are cold. 

You can practice modals of likelihood HERE and HERE.

Past modals
What are the past forms of modals? 

In the previous chapter we looked at the past forms of modals and some substitutes:

Can -> could
May -> was/were allowed to
Must -> had to

In addition we also use SHOULD, WOULD and COULD + HAVE + III form to talk about things that did not really happen in the past.

I should have called sooner. (=I regret that I did not call sooner) OLEKSIN PIDANUD HELISTAMA
I would have called if I had had time. (=I did not call because I did not have time) OLEKSIN HELISTANUD
I could have called you earlier. (=It was possible, but I did not do it) OLEKSIN VÕINUD HELISTADA

You can read more about this type of past modals HERE.

Modals of deduction that are used to express possibility, probability and certainty are made up of MODAL + (NOT) + HAVE + III form (past participle)
  • CAN'T (only used in the negative form) is used to express past certainty.
           It can't have been that late! (=it is impossible that it was so late).
  • COULD and MIGHT are used to express past probability or unreal possibility (something that did not actually happen).
           I might have seen him yesterday (=maybe I saw him, it is possible)
           It could not have been him! (=it is impossible that it was him)
           It could have been much worse (=but it wasn't) 
           I might have been killed! (= it was possible, but actually I wasn't)
  • MAY is used to express past probability.
           She may have missed the bus (=it is possible that she missed the bus)
           She may not have seen you (=it is possible that she did not see you)
  • MUST is used to express past certainty.
           You must have left your keys at home (=I am certain you left them there)
           You must not have locked the door (=I am certain you didn't do it)

All these past modals are used to show how certain we are that something did or did not happen in the past.
