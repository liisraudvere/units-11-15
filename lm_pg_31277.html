Grammar: conditional sentences

What are conditional sentences and how do we form them?

Conditional sentences are used to talk about how one factors or situations leads to another, to discuss the consequences or any real or hypothetical situations. There are five types of conditional sentences in English, very similarly to Estonian.

If you feel you are familiar with the form and use of conditional sentences, you can scroll down to the bottom of the page for the review and practice exercises.
If you need more information and practice, please click on the sections below for a more detailed look at each type of conditional sentences.
Conditionals type 0 and 1
What are conditional sentences? How do we form them? What are the rules?

Conditional sentences (tingimuslaused) are used to talk about a real, hypothetical or unreal condition and then a connected result.

There are 4 main types of conditional sentences, plus some mixed types.

The first type of conditional sentences is 0 conditionals, or present real conditionals.

We use the 0 conditionals to talk about facts, things that are generally true.

The 0 conditional is made up of WHEN/IF + present simple on one side and the present simple on the other side.

For example: When you heat ice, it melts. If I have time, I clean my rifle. = Sometimes I have time and then I like to clean my rifle.
The 1st conditional is used to talk about realistic future situations, so it is also called the future real conditional.

The 1st conditional is made up of IF + present simple on one side and the future simple on the other side.

For example: If I study hard, I will get a good grade. = I am not sure if I will study hard and so get a good grade.

Conditionals type 2
The 2nd conditional or type 2 conditional is used to talk about hypothetical present or future situations.

This conditional is also called the present unreal conditional.

The second conditional uses IF + past simple on one side and WOULD + infinitive on the other side.

For example: If I had a million dollars, I would buy a house. = I don't have a million dollars at the moment. But, theoretically I could have that much money and the hypothetical result of that would be buying a house.

Conditionals type 3
The third conditional is used to describe an unreal past situation and its impossible past consequence. 

This type is also called the past unreal conditional.

The 3rd conditional talks about a past event that did not happen and its result that did not happen.

It is made up of IF and the past perfect on one side and would and the present perfect on the other side.

For example: If I had studied for the test, I would not have failed. = I did not study and I failed.

Mixed conditionals
In addition to the previously mentioned types of conditional sentences, there are also mixed conditionals.

We can mix the 2nd and 3rd conditionals to talk about the present results of past events or the past results of present events.
